# Startup

## Scope Defaults
Treat startup, founder, launch, and MVP requests as a move from uncertain idea to testable market evidence. Start with the smallest useful customer segment, the pain being solved, the promise, the first offer, and the next reversible launch step. Prefer interviews, landing pages, concierge delivery, waitlists, and manual fulfillment before software buildout unless the user has already proven demand.

Default scope includes problem framing, ideal customer profile, positioning, MVP slice, risk list, launch checklist, evidence plan, and a simple operating cadence. Exclude fundraising, legal formation, paid ads, or production engineering until there is explicit context or approval.

## Checklist
- State the assumed customer, problem, and outcome in one plain sentence.
- Identify the riskiest assumption: demand, willingness to pay, delivery feasibility, distribution, or retention.
- Define the MVP as the smallest promise a real customer can accept or reject.
- Pick one acquisition channel that the founder can execute this week.
- Create a proof plan with interview targets, landing page copy, pricing test, or manual pilot.
- List launch assets: offer page, onboarding note, customer feedback form, follow-up sequence, and support path.
- Mark blockers that require user approval, money, legal advice, or access credentials.
- End with the next concrete action and the evidence that will show whether it worked.

## Pitfalls
- Building a full product before testing the painful use case.
- Asking the founder to choose technical architecture before confirming the offer.
- Treating compliments, likes, or vague interest as demand.
- Launching to everyone instead of a narrow reachable segment.
- Hiding operational work that makes the first customers successful.
- Spending money on tools, ads, contractors, or incorporation without explicit approval.

## Success Looks Like
The founder has a clear customer, a sharp promise, a small MVP, and a test that can produce evidence within days. The next step is reversible, cheap, and tied to a measurable signal such as booked calls, qualified waitlist joins, pilot commitments, paid preorders, activation, or retained usage. Unknowns are named honestly instead of being filled with guesses.

## Examples
Example 1: "Launch my AI study app." Infer students preparing for exams as the first segment, draft a landing page promise, define a manual quiz-generation MVP, create a 10-interview script, and set a one-week success target before recommending a build.

Example 2: "Do everything for my founder idea." Start with problem, customer, offer, risks, and a first validation sprint. Ask at most one blocker question if the target customer is completely unknowable from available context.

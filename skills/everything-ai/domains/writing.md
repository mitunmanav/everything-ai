# Writing

## Scope Defaults
Treat write, draft, edit, blog, email, essay, report, copy, and content requests as move-from-blank-to-usable-text. Start by identifying the audience, purpose, and tone. On new drafts: structure first, then write. On edits: preserve the author's voice; improve clarity and concision. On emails: professional unless context says otherwise. Exclude brand strategy, SEO audits, or paid content distribution unless explicitly approved.

Default scope includes: audience and purpose identification, tone selection, structure outline, first draft or edit pass, headline or subject line suggestion, call-to-action recommendation, and a brief note on what was assumed about audience and tone.

## Checklist
- Identify: who is the reader, what should they feel or do after reading, what tone fits.
- State the core message in one sentence before drafting.
- Build a structure (intro, body, close) before writing full paragraphs.
- Write the first draft. Prioritize clarity over cleverness.
- Check: does each paragraph serve the core message? Cut what doesn't.
- Check: is the opening sentence strong enough to keep reading?
- Suggest a subject line (for email), title (for articles), or headline (for ads).
- Note assumptions about audience, tone, and length.

## Pitfalls
- Writing for the wrong audience (too formal, too casual, wrong expertise level).
- Burying the main point in the third paragraph.
- Using jargon the reader doesn't know.
- Editing the voice out — preserve how the user sounds if editing their existing text.
- Missing the call to action — every piece of writing should have a clear next step.

## Success Looks Like
The text is clear, on-tone, and serves its purpose. The reader knows what to think, feel, or do after reading. The opening grabs attention. The structure is logical. The language matches the audience. Assumptions about tone and audience are stated so the user can correct them.

## Examples
Example 1: "Write my email." Infer the recipient type (colleague, client, manager) from context. Draft a clear, professional email with subject line. State assumption: assumed professional tone, adjust if too formal.

Example 2: "Edit my essay." Read the full draft first. Preserve the author's voice. Improve sentence clarity, remove filler words, strengthen the opening, tighten the conclusion. Report: what changed, what was kept, what still needs work.

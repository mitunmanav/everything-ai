# Personal Productivity

## Scope Defaults
Treat tasks, notes, schedule, planning, and week requests as a prioritization and execution system, not a generic motivation exercise. Start by organizing commitments, deadlines, energy constraints, and next actions. Prefer a small plan the user can actually follow today over a complete life-management overhaul.

Default scope includes inbox capture, task clarification, calendar constraints, priority ranking, time blocks, delegation or deferral options, reminders, and an end-of-day review loop. Exclude contacting people, booking paid services, deleting notes, or moving calendar events unless the user explicitly approves those actions.

## Checklist
- Collect visible tasks, deadlines, meetings, notes, and open loops from available context.
- Convert vague items into next actions with an owner and target date.
- Separate urgent, important, waiting, someday, and blocked work.
- Reserve fixed calendar commitments before assigning flexible tasks.
- Pick a realistic daily top three and a weekly outcome list.
- Batch shallow work and protect focus blocks for high-value work.
- Add buffers for travel, recovery, admin, and spillover.
- End with a review ritual that updates the plan from actual progress.

## Pitfalls
- Creating an overloaded schedule that assumes perfect focus all day.
- Treating every captured task as equally important.
- Planning without checking calendar reality.
- Splitting work into tiny fragments that prevent deep progress.
- Hiding blocked tasks instead of naming the missing decision, context, or permission.
- Changing calendars, sending messages, or deleting notes without approval.

## Success Looks Like
The user has a short, credible plan for today and a clear view of the week. High-impact work is protected, deadlines are visible, low-value work is deferred or batched, and blocked items have a named next step. The plan is easy to adjust when new tasks arrive.

## Examples
Example 1: "Plan my week." Group commitments by deadline and importance, place fixed events first, choose three weekly outcomes, reserve focus blocks, and leave open capacity for surprises.

Example 2: "Organize my notes and tasks." Turn notes into projects, next actions, waiting items, and references. Keep original notes intact unless the user approves cleanup or deletion.

## Frameworks

- **GTD (Getting Things Done)**: Capture everything. Clarify next actions. Organize by context. Review weekly.
- **MIT (Most Important Task)**: Identify 1–3 MITs each morning before checking messages. Do them first.
- **Time-blocking**: Assign work to specific calendar slots. Protect focus blocks from meetings.
